# Email Outreach

A structured approach to writing cold emails that get opened, read, and responded to.

---

## Core Principle

**Cold email is not about you — it's about them. Lead with value, not with your needs.**

Every recipient asks: "Why should I care?" Answer that in the first two sentences.

---

## Phase 1: Recipient Research

### Know Who You're Writing To

```
RESEARCH CHECKLIST

□ What do they do? (Role, company, research area)
□ What have they done recently? (Papers, projects, talks)
□ What do they care about? (Interests, values, goals)
□ What are they working on now? (Current focus)
□ How can you help them? (Value you can provide)
□ Do you have mutual connections? (Warm intro opportunity)
```

### Find the Hook

```
HOOK SOURCES

Recent work:
"I read your paper on X and was particularly struck by Y..."

Shared interest:
"We both presented at [conference] on related topics..."

Mutual connection:
"[Name] suggested I reach out to you about..."

Their stated goals:
"I saw your post about wanting to learn more about X..."

Common problem:
"I noticed you and I both work on X, and I've been thinking about Y..."
```

## Phase 2: Email Structure

### The 5-Line Framework

```
LINE 1: Context (why you're writing)
LINE 2: Hook (why they should care)
LINE 3: Value (what's in it for them)
LINE 4: Ask (what you want)
LINE 5: Out (easy exit)
```

### Line-by-Line Breakdown

**Line 1: Context**
```
GOOD:
"I'm a PhD student at MIT working on water resource modeling."
"I'm building a tool for academic researchers."
"I came across your work through [specific paper/talk]."

BAD:
"My name is John and I'm reaching out."
"I hope this email finds you well."
"I'm writing to you today because..."
```

**Line 2: Hook**
```
GOOD:
"Your approach to X directly addresses a problem I've been struggling with."
"I noticed you recently published on Y — I'm working on something complementary."
"Your framework for Z could save us months of work."

BAD:
"I really admire your work."
"I've been following your career."
"You're an expert in the field."
```

**Line 3: Value**
```
GOOD:
"I have data that might help validate your recent hypothesis."
"I'd be happy to share our implementation — it might save you time."
"I can offer [specific thing] in exchange for [specific thing]."

BAD:
"I'd love to pick your brain."
"Can I have 15 minutes of your time?"
"I'd really appreciate your help."
```

**Line 4: Ask**
```
GOOD:
"Would you be open to a 15-minute call next week?"
"Could I send you a 2-page summary of our approach?"
"Would you be willing to review a draft?"

BAD:
"Can we chat sometime?"
"Let me know if you're interested."
"What do you think?"
```

**Line 5: Out**
```
GOOD:
"No worries if this isn't a fit — I know you're busy."
"If now's not a good time, I completely understand."
"Either way, thanks for the great work you're doing."

BAD:
[No out — makes saying no awkward]
"Looking forward to your response."
"Please reply by Friday."
```

## Phase 3: Subject Lines

### Subject Line Principles

```
GOOD SUBJECT LINES ARE:
□ Specific (not generic)
□ Relevant to recipient
□ Honest (not clickbait)
□ Short (under 50 characters)
□ Actionable (implies what's inside)
```

### Subject Line Templates

**Academic outreach:**
```
"Question about your [paper/topic]"
"Collaboration idea: [specific topic]"
"[Mutual connection] suggested I reach out"
"Following up on [conference/talk]"
```

**Product/Service outreach:**
```
"[Specific result] for [company type] like [recipient company]"
"Idea for [specific problem they have]"
"Quick question about [their recent work]"
"[Mutual connection] + [specific topic]"
```

**Networking:**
```
"Fellow [school/alumni] working on [topic]"
"Met at [event] — [specific topic]"
"[Specific topic] question from a [your role]"
```

## Phase 4: Email Types

### Academic Cold Email

```
Subject: Question about your recent paper on hydrological modeling

Hi Dr. [Name],

I'm a PhD student at [University] working on machine learning 
approaches to flood prediction.

Your recent paper on [specific paper] directly addresses a challenge 
I've been facing with [specific problem]. Your approach to 
[specific method] is particularly elegant.

I have a dataset of [description] that might help validate your 
findings on [specific claim]. Would you be interested in 
collaborating, or could I send you a brief summary of our results?

No pressure if you're too busy — I know how demanding research is.

Best,
[Your name]
[Your title]
[Your university]
[Link to your work, if relevant]
```

### Product Outreach

```
Subject: Idea for reducing your support ticket volume

Hi [Name],

I'm [Your name], founder of [Company]. We help [target customer] 
with [specific problem].

I noticed [Company] recently [specific observation — hiring support, 
public issue, etc.]. We've helped similar companies reduce support 
tickets by 40% through [specific approach].

Would you be open to seeing a 2-minute demo of how this works? 
Happy to share exactly how [similar company] achieved [result].

If this isn't a priority right now, no worries at all.

[Your name]
```

### Networking Request

```
Subject: Fellow [School] alum working on [topic]

Hi [Name],

I'm a [year] graduate from [School] now working on [topic] at 
[Company/Institution].

I saw your profile and noticed you're [what they do]. I'm 
particularly interested in [specific area] and would love to learn 
about your experience with [specific thing].

Would you be open to a brief coffee chat or call? I'm happy to 
work around your schedule.

If you're too busy, I completely understand.

Best,
[Your name]
[LinkedIn profile or portfolio]
```

## Phase 5: Follow-Up Strategy

### When to Follow Up

```
FOLLOW-UP TIMELINE

Day 1: Initial email
Day 5: First follow-up (if no response)
Day 12: Second follow-up (if no response)
Day 30: Final follow-up (if no response)

Then: Move on. Don't be a stalker.
```

### Follow-Up Templates

**First follow-up (add value):**
```
Subject: Re: [Original subject]

Hi [Name],

I wanted to follow up on my email from last week about [topic].

Since then, I [new development — published something, found new 
data, had an insight]. I thought you might find this particularly 
relevant given your work on [their work].

Still happy to [original offer] if you're interested.

[Your name]
```

**Second follow-up (polite bump):**
```
Subject: Re: [Original subject]

Hi [Name],

I know you're busy, so I'll keep this brief.

I'm still very interested in [topic] and would value your 
perspective. If now isn't a good time, I completely understand.

[Your name]
```

**Final follow-up (graceful exit):**
```
Subject: Re: [Original subject]

Hi [Name],

I wanted to try one last time. I'm still interested in [topic], 
but I don't want to clutter your inbox.

If you're open to connecting in the future, I'll leave the ball 
in your court.

Thanks for your time,
[Your name]
```

## Phase 6: Response Handling

### When They Respond Positively

```
DO:
□ Respond quickly (within 24 hours)
□ Be specific about next steps
□ Make it easy for them (suggest times, send calendar link)
□ Prepare thoroughly for any meeting
□ Follow through on any promises

DON'T:
□ Delay your response
□ Be vague about next steps
□ Make them do the scheduling work
□ Waste their time in the meeting
□ Forget to follow up after
```

### When They Respond Negatively

```
RESPONSE: "Thanks, but I'm not interested."

YOUR REPLY:
"Thanks for letting me know, [Name]. I appreciate your time. 
Best of luck with [their work]."

→ Leave the door open. Don't burn bridges.
```

### When They Don't Respond

```
DON'T:
□ Take it personally
□ Send angry follow-ups
□ Try to guilt them
□ Keep emailing indefinitely

DO:
□ Assume they're busy
□ Move on to other prospects
□ Keep the door open for future contact
□ Learn from the experience
```

## Common Pitfalls

### Content Pitfalls

1. **Too long**: More than 5-6 sentences
   - Fix: Ruthlessly edit. Every word must earn its place.

2. **Too vague**: "I'd love to chat" without specifics
   - Fix: Specific ask, specific value, specific topic

3. **All about you**: Listing your credentials
   - Fix: Lead with them, their work, their interests

4. **No clear ask**: Leaving them to figure out what you want
   - Fix: One specific, easy request

5. **Generic flattery**: "I'm a big fan of your work"
   - Fix: Specific, genuine observation about their work

### Process Pitfalls

1. **Mass emailing**: Same email to everyone
   - Fix: Personalize every email

2. **No research**: Writing without knowing who they are
   - Fix: Spend 5 minutes researching before writing

3. **Premature follow-up**: Following up in 24 hours
   - Fix: Wait 5-7 days minimum

4. **Giving up too soon**: One email, no response, moving on
   - Fix: Follow up 2-3 times over 3-4 weeks
